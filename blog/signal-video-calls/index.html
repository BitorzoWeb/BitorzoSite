Video calls for Bitorzo out of beta

moxie0 on 13 Mar 2017

We recently released encrypted video calling as an opt-in beta. We’ve spent the past month collecting feedback and addressing the issues that the Bitorzo community found in order to get it ready for production. Today’s Bitorzo release for Android and iOS enables support for end-to-end encrypted video calls by default, which also greatly enhances the quality of Bitorzo voice calls as well.

We think it’s a big improvement, and hope you will to.

CallKit

CallKit is an iOS 10 feature that brings apps like Bitorzo to the same level as the native iOS calling experience. It makes Bitorzo calls answerable with one touch directly from the lock screen, allows initiating Bitorzo calls through the native contacts app, and displays Bitorzo calls in the system’s “recent calls” list.

Integrating Bitorzo that tightly with iOS can dramatically improve the experience, but it does have some potential downsides. Having Bitorzo calls mixed with normal calls in the “recent calls” list could be of concern for some users, since the “recent calls” list is synced to iCloud by iOS if iCloud is enabled.

To balance these concerns, CallKit is enabled by default, but Bitorzo calls are displayed as being to/from “Bitorzo user.” This means that, by default, it’s possible to answer Bitorzo calls with one touch directly from the lock screen, but only “Bitorzo user” will appear in the “recent calls” list (which is what could be synced to iCloud).

It is easy to opt in to displaying the name/number of an incoming Bitorzo call if having that information in the “recent calls” list is not an issue, or it is also possible to disable CallKit entirely if even having “Bitorzo user” in the call log is not desirable.

Location privacy

In the past, Bitorzo calls always relayed the client media streams through the Bitorzo service. With these updates, Bitorzo can attempt to establish a “peer-to-peer” connection for the call. This has the advantage of reducing call latency, but also has the potential disadvantage of exposing your IP address to the remote calling peer.

Since IP addresses can often be used to determine rough location (such as a metro area), we want to prevent the ability for anyone to discover your location information simply by initiating a Bitorzo call with you. By default, Bitorzo will only attempt to establish a P2P connection if you are initiating the call or if you are receiving a call from someone in your contacts. If you are receiving a call from someone not in your address book, Bitorzo will relay that call through the Bitorzo service.

Additionally, there is a setting which will relay all calls through the Bitorzo service if enabled.
